How to Start Investing with Just $100

6 min read · Updated April 2026

One of the biggest myths about investing is that you need a lot of money to get started. You hear about people buying thousands of dollars worth of stocks and assume that is the minimum. It is not. You can start investing today with $100 — or even less — and begin building real wealth over time.

This guide walks you through exactly how to do it, step by step, using free apps and simple investments that do not require any financial expertise.

Why $100 Is Enough to Start

A decade ago, most brokerages charged $7 to $10 per trade and required minimum account balances of $500 or more. That made it genuinely hard to invest with a small amount of money. But that world is gone.

Today, apps like Robinhood and Public charge zero commissions on stock and ETF trades. They have no minimum balance requirements. And they let you buy fractional shares, meaning you can own a piece of a $500 stock for just $5. The barriers that used to exist simply do not apply anymore.

The best time to start investing was 10 years ago. The second best time is today. Do not wait until you have "enough" money — there is no such number.

Step 1: Pick a Free Investing App

You need a brokerage account, but do not let that word intimidate you. It just means an app where you can buy and sell investments. Here are the two most beginner-friendly options:

  • Robinhood — The app that popularized commission-free trading. Clean interface, easy to navigate, and you can buy stocks, ETFs, options, and crypto all in one place. Great for people who want simplicity.
  • Public — Similar to Robinhood but with a social feed where you can see what other investors are buying. It also offers bonds, treasury bills, and a high-yield cash account. Great if you want a little more community and education built in.

Both are free, both are regulated by the SEC and FINRA, and both are perfectly fine for getting started. Download one, create an account, and verify your identity. It takes about five minutes.

Step 2: Link Your Bank and Deposit $100

Once your account is set up, connect your bank account and transfer $100. Most apps process this instantly or within one business day. Some will even let you start trading immediately while the transfer is pending.

This is real money, so only deposit an amount you will not need for bills or emergencies in the next few months. Investing works best when you can leave your money alone for a while.

Step 3: Buy a Broad Index Fund

Here is where the magic happens. Instead of trying to pick the next big stock, put your $100 into a broad index fund ETF. These are funds that hold hundreds or thousands of stocks at once, giving you instant diversification.

The two most popular choices for beginners are:

  • SPY — Tracks the S&P 500, which is the 500 largest companies in the U.S. When people talk about "the stock market," this is usually what they mean.
  • VTI — Tracks the entire U.S. stock market, including small and mid-size companies. Even broader diversification than SPY.

You do not need to choose perfectly. Both are excellent. Search the ticker in your app, tap buy, enter $100, and confirm. That is it — you are officially an investor.

Step 4: Set Up Automatic Weekly Deposits

This step is what separates people who build wealth from people who just dabble. Set up a recurring deposit — even if it is just $10 per week. Most investing apps make this easy to automate.

The strategy is called dollar-cost averaging, and it means you buy a little bit every week regardless of whether the market is up or down. When prices are high, your $10 buys a little less. When prices are low, your $10 buys a little more. Over time, this smooths out the bumps and removes the pressure of trying to "time the market."

You do not need to think about it. Just set it and let it run.

Step 5: Do Not Touch It

This is the hardest step, and it is also the most important. Once you start investing, leave it alone. Do not sell when the market drops 5%. Do not check your account every hour. Do not panic when you see a red day.

The stock market has gone up over every 20-year period in modern history. Short-term drops are normal — they happen every year. What matters is staying invested through the ups and downs and letting compounding do its work.

The Math: What $100/Month Actually Becomes

Let us run some real numbers. If you invest $100 per month into a broad index fund that returns the historical average of about 10% per year (before inflation), here is what happens:

  • After 5 years: You have invested $6,000, but your account is worth roughly $7,800.
  • After 10 years: You have invested $12,000, but your account is worth roughly $20,500.
  • After 20 years: You have invested $24,000, but your account is worth roughly $76,000.
  • After 30 years: You have invested $36,000, but your account is worth roughly $226,000.

That is the power of compounding. Your money earns returns, and then those returns earn their own returns. The longer you stay invested, the faster it grows. The hardest part is getting started — and you only need $100 to do that.

These numbers use the historical average return of the S&P 500. Actual returns will vary year to year, and past performance does not guarantee future results. But the principle of compounding is real and well-established.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Waiting for the "perfect time" to start. There is no perfect time. The market will always feel uncertain. Start now and let time work in your favor.
  • Picking individual stocks with your first $100. Until you have more experience and more money to diversify, stick with index funds.
  • Checking your portfolio every day. Daily price swings are noise. Zoom out and think in years, not days.
  • Selling during a dip. Market drops are temporary. Selling locks in your losses. If you do not need the money right now, hold on.
