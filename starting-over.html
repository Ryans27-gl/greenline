For women starting over

You're not behind. You're just starting now.

A gentle, honest guide to understanding investing and taking control of your own money — written for women who were never taught, and are tired of being talked down to.

If you're here because your ex handled all the finances, or because you've spent decades focused on raising kids and running a household while someone else managed the money — you are not alone, and you are not starting from zero. You already know how to budget, plan, and protect the people you love. That's the hardest part. Everything else is just vocabulary.

Why This Feels So Hard

The finance world has a confidence problem. It speaks in acronyms, assumes you already know what a 401k is, and puts the information behind jargon walls that make you feel stupid for asking. You are not stupid. The industry designed it that way on purpose — it keeps you dependent on advisors who charge for answers that should be free.

Here's the truth: most of investing is boring, simple math. A few key concepts. Some patience. That's it. You can absolutely do this on your own, and once you understand the basics, you'll wonder why anyone ever made it feel so intimidating.

Where to Actually Start

If you're overwhelmed, don't try to learn everything at once. Start with these five steps — in order, one at a time. There's no rush.

Step 1

Know what you have

Before you invest anything, make a simple list of every account in your name: checking, savings, retirement accounts (401k, IRA), brokerage accounts, and any settlements you received. Write down the balance of each. This is the foundation. You can't plan without knowing the numbers.

Step 2

Understand what a retirement account actually is

A 401k or IRA is just a special kind of investment account with tax benefits. The money inside is invested in stocks and bonds — those go up and down with the market. If you had a 401k through your ex's employer that was split in the divorce, that money is probably still invested and quietly growing. You can move it to your own IRA and manage it yourself.

Step 3

Learn what "the market" really is

When people say "the stock market went up today," they mean a collection of 500 big companies (the S&P 500) collectively became more valuable. You can own a tiny piece of all 500 at once by buying a single thing called an index fund. It's the simplest, safest way to grow money over time — and it's exactly what most professional advisors recommend for beginners.

Step 4

Start with a small, boring account

You don't need thousands of dollars. Open a free brokerage account (we recommend something beginner-friendly like Public or Fidelity) and put in $50 or $100. Buy one index fund like SPY or VTI. Watch it for a few weeks. That's it. You're now officially an investor. The biggest lesson is that most days, nothing exciting happens — and that's exactly how it's supposed to work.

Step 5

Read the news, but don't trade on it

Once you own a little, the news starts making sense because it's about your money. Reading market news daily (in plain English, not jargon) will teach you more in three months than any textbook. The goal isn't to react to every headline — it's to build a quiet understanding of how the world affects your portfolio over years.

What Not to Do

Don't pay anyone who promises high returns. Real investing is slow and boring. If someone is pressuring you to put money into something complicated, they're probably trying to take some of it.

Don't feel stupid for asking basic questions. There is no "beginner tax" in the real world — the only thing that costs you is not asking.

Don't compare yourself to people your age. Someone who started investing in their 20s had 30 years of compounding. You didn't. That's okay. Starting today is still the best thing you can do.

Don't wait until you "feel ready." Nobody feels ready. The readiness comes from doing, not from studying.
